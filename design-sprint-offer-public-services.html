About the project

Working as a service designer within the Metropolis of Lyon for one year in a project team, I participated in the preparation of a design sprint. For 18 hours, we designed and prototyped service concepts through mobile applications in order to clarify the service offer provided by the metropolis Trabool and Toodego services.

Problem

The digital services offered to users of the Lyon metropolitan area are geared towards the use of Toodego for administrative procedures and the Trabool service for mobility in transport and cultural offers. These services are built in a silo, because the current and future roadmaps are not known and shared with the whole team, which leads to proposals of sometimes similar services: bus timetables, service proposals, same partners. Following feedback from partners and users, expressing their lack of understanding of the services, the project managers decided to find a solution by organising a sprint design. The design agency Nod-A was commissioned to set up the workshop. Project manager Erwan Leluron invited the in-house designers at the metropolis, Alexandra Douine and myself, to accompany him. Our role was to design the organisation of the sprint with the agency's designers.

Sprint design is an innovative workshop method based on problem-solving through experimentation, modelling and rapid idea-hypothesis testing over 5 days with very little means.

1. Preparation of the sprint

Setting objectives

Together with the internet team, we set the objectives upstream and downstream of the project during co-creation sessions. We set ourselves the goal of:

  • improving and clarifying value propositions and service offers;
  • help partner users to better differentiate services and make journeys between them more fluid (switching from one service to another);
  • communicate clearly about the services proposed;
  • have a common vision.

Identifying the problem

The sprint takes place over two days, so the issue was chosen during meetings with the project managers, the manager, the designers and the sprint design agency. Various issues that the project teams of the Trabool and Toodego departments came up with, such as :

  • The service offering of the products is not known or shared by all project managers and project teams;
  • The services offer some similar functionalities;
  • The BtoB offer: the partners do not always understand the link between the different services (and receive several requests from project managers);
  • The BtoC offer: users get lost in the services (example: there is the same offer for Velo'v).

4 possible scenarios

Four hypotheses of scenarios have been put forward to determine the scope of the solution that the ideation will lead to. These scenarios represent whether the services should merge completely, on certain services or no longer cover the same services.

  1. Merger of the two services.
  2. Dividing the services in two.
  3. One product sucks certain services from the other.
  4. Total separation of services.

Organising the project

For the preparation of the sprint, the design agency was in charge of designing the tools of the workshop (persona, course, canvas) and Alexandra and myself were in charge of making the ecosystem of current services as well as the business model. Other meetings were held in order to clarify the problems and to develop the workshop that Nod-A proposed to us.

Value proposition of services

Before the sprint, I proposed to draw up a business model and value proposition for each department concerned with the project managers (Toodego, Trabool, Only'moov, Grand Lyon Connect) in order to identify possible repetitions or subjects to be clarified. They will then be presented to the participants during the sprint.

The value proposition design and business model serve to identify the value created by a product or service that customers need and benefit from.

I therefore organised workshops with the project managers for the development of new service proposals. This exercise was the way to identify opportunities during the workshop.

2. Design Sprint

Â 

Background

The sprint design took place over two days with 20 participants (project managers and experts) divided into two teams. Alexandra and I, in-house designers, were shared in each team in order to bring a design point of view and content to the follow-up of the projects we have.

Start with the system analysis

After a presentation of the services business model, each team was asked to link similar information and areas of overlap found in other business models in order to identify duplications, but also to identify unclear elements. We were able to see common partners, services and information (e.g. air quality, traffic information) and common functionalities (each service includes a dashboard). The value propositions have similarities for Toodego and Trabool, and are no less to be specified.

Â 

The teams also noted all the interactions between the products in order to add "points of suffering" maps that identify questions about the ecosystem, duplication and misunderstanding of services.Â 

Creating new user journey

The identification of ecosystem irritants and similarities in the business models served as a basis for creating user routes with the help of persona, according to the hypotheses of project scenarios (merging, sharing, aspiration, separation). The two teams produced three scenarios, each one annotated with "points of suffering" and questioning on all the pathways created.

Which hypotheses to choose?

Each participant votes for the scenario that seems most plausible with the least amount of questioning and irritation, so that each team can prototype a solution for the chosen scenario.

Â 

Team 1 having chosen the "one smaller, one bigger" scenario, one product sucks certain services from the other, i.e. one project takes certain functionalities from the other service, and team 2 the "one smaller, one bigger" scenario, one product sucks certain services from the other, i.e. one project takes certain functionalities from the other service, and team 2 the "total separation of services" scenario.

I personally chose hypothesis three, which is to separate the services, because it seems to me that dividing the services makes it possible to better reflect on how they interact together and to determine the fields of action of each one (similar ecosystem). This division would be more distinct in terms of what each service proposes for users and partners, as well as for project managers to act from an operational point of view.

Materialize the user journey

The assumptions of the selected scenarios were used as a basis for the preparation of new usage scenarios. Based on this, the teams designed different supports in order to project themselves into the solutions:

  • Product sheets for the services imagined;
  • User journeys;
  • A new ecosystem integrated with other products and services;
  • A road map in order to have a medium and long term vision up to 2025 listing all the functionalities over time;
  • A summary "Our principle" sheet.

Final presentation

In order to present the solutions we have found and to envisage the continuation after the sprint, we pitched the prototypes to the service director and final decision-maker of the sprint. The feedback he has been able to give is about the separation of the services and the silencing of the solutions. He thinks that the services should not be separated, but crossed so that the users move from one service to the other and are in daily use.

3. After the sprint

Â 

Analysis of returns

Despite this, what can we do? The bulk of the work was done over two days thanks to the collective intelligence and expertise of each person, creating a lot of material to build on, which had to be reworked and discussed with a cold mind. Moreover, the important thing in sprint design is also to test the prototypes in order to evaluate the user feedback and above all to validate or invalidate the hypotheses with prototypes. At this stage, we don't know if the ideas can lead to real service.

Team sharing

We then organised meetings with the project managers to discuss the solutions implemented and the follow-up and with the decision-makers in order to have a strategic vision. This process would end with tests or experiments with a panel of experienced users.

In the end, however, decision-makers will choose not to go for one of the solutions imagined. They were not satisfied with the results and preferred to continue as they had left, although there were inconsistencies and overlaps in services. We could not go through the whole process and test the ideas.

From there, we still held meetings with the directors and decision-makers in order to collect the positive and negative opinions of each service proposal, but also the strategies and objectives of the Metropolis and the role that the projects should have. We used the meetings to bring out the rules of obligations, constraints and wishes in relation to the service.

Creation of a "project kit

We have designed project sheets for project managers to orient themselves according to the strategy of the Metropolis, listing the mandatory and desirable rules of the services.

Conclusion

Â 

The workshop allowed me to insist on elements of the service to be clarified. It also allowed me to affirm the role of design and in-house designers. I was able to propose tools for analysing services and bring the expertise I had acquired on projects during the year during the sprint. This bubble of imagination created by the workshop and collective intelligence gives a group dynamic trying to find a common solution to the problems it encounters. The continuation of the workshop is a crucial moment, because it is necessary to be able to take up the elements conceived during the workshop to continue well the continuation. Even if the project has not yet come to fruition, I had the opportunity to set up a design approach at the source of the problem and to point it out. The decision-making process is very complex and we are still struggling to get information from the field back to the decision-makers.

This project was the end of my journey within the Metropolis and showed me the complexity of implementing projects for users, as well as the lack of testing and the brakes on institutional change. This project combining social problems, political communication and the interest of the partners reinforced my belief that we should have integrated the final decision-makers further upstream in the design process. Finally, the Metropolis has been enriched with a design culture thanks to this project, even though I expected much more and the sprint did not lead to an imagined solution, but served to improve project practice.
